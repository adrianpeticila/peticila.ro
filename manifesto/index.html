P.007 — manifesto

what i believe
about marketing.

20+ years in this industry. here's what i've actually learned — about strategy, brand, and why activity is usually the enemy of progress.

on marketing

activity is not progress.

most marketing fails because the people running it have confused activity with progress.

they measure what's easy to measure. they optimize what's already working marginally. they mistake a full calendar for a working strategy.

twenty years in this industry. the pattern hasn't changed. what has changed: the tools got faster, the excuses got better, and the gap between companies that understand positioning and companies that don't got wider.

"a full calendar is not a working strategy."

on strategy

strategy is not a plan.

a plan is how you execute. strategy is the answer to a harder question: why would someone choose you instead of the thing they're already doing?

most companies don't have a strategy. they have a set of tactics organized into slides. the slides usually have a framework on them. nobody ever got a customer from a framework.

i've built marketing functions from scratch, rebuilt ones that had gone wrong, and led teams in b2b markets that don't respond to generic playbooks. the pattern is the same everywhere: clarity of positioning beats volume of activity, every time.

on brand

brand is the reputation you have when you're not in the room.

not your logo. not your tone of voice guidelines. what people say when someone asks about you and they're being honest.

most b2b brand work gets this backwards. it focuses on what the company wants to say rather than what the buyer needs to believe. the gap between those two things is where most marketing budgets disappear.

getting that right is not a creative problem. it's a strategic one.

"brand is not what you say about yourself. it's what they say when you leave the room."

on how i work

20+ years. still not bored.

i don't do everything. i do positioning, brand strategy, and marketing architecture for companies and people who are good at what they do but unclear about how to make that matter to the right audience.

3 cmo-track roles. 10+ brands built from positioning up. b2b tech, professional services, enterprise software. the problems look different but the diagnostic questions are the same.

still more interested in the hard problem than the comfortable one.
